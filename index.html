    // the problem
    
      
    
        
    SMBs and field-service companies were drowning in unlogged sales calls. Reps would hang up, forget half the conversation, and never make it into the CRM. Pipeline visibility was a guess. Sales managers couldn't tell which leads were hot, which deals were slipping, or which reps needed coaching — and the manual logging tax on reps was eating 30+ minutes per day per person.
    
        
    The market was full of "AI dialers" but none of them solved the actual problem: the structured intelligence trapped inside every conversation.
    
      
    
    
    

    
    
      
    // my role
    
      
    
        
    Founder in Residence / Head Product Manager. Joined as the founding product hire and owned the full product motion: roadmap, build, GTM. The team was small, scrappy, and shipping every week.

    • Pushes timeline, integration, blockers, and next-steps directly into the customer's CRM

    • Flags pipeline-changing moments ("they mentioned a Q2 deadline") for sales-manager review

    // what I learned
    
      
    
        
    Latency was the product, not the feature. We initially optimized for transcription accuracy and assumed users would tolerate a 2-3 second delay. They didn't. Sub-second response time turned out to be the difference between "useful tool" and "actually used during the call." If I did this again, I'd start with a latency budget before any model selection.
    
        
    Outbound is a research function before it's a sales function. Treating each weekly campaign as an experiment — with a clear hypothesis, single-variable change, and pre-committed success threshold — felt slower at first but produced compounding learnings the team could act on. By week 12 we had a documented playbook other team members could run.
    
        
    Founder-as-PM is a real role. What I underestimated was how much of "PM" at this stage is sequencing — knowing when to push for product polish vs. ship-and-iterate vs. step back to GTM. I'd write that sequencing logic down explicitly going forward, instead of running it from instinct.

    // the problem
    
      
    
        
    Most student game projects die in the pivot or never ship. Scope creeps. Members lose interest. The vision drifts. Six months in, the team has 30 conflicting prototypes and no game.
    
        
    We wanted to actually launch on Steam — which means real scope discipline, real milestones, and a team that doesn't fall apart in week six.
    
      
    
    
    

    
    
      
    // my role
    
      
    
        
    Project Lead, UI/UX Lead, Audio Lead, Narrative Lead. Coordinating an 8-person cross-functional team while also owning the player-facing experience end-to-end.
    
      
    
    
    

    
    
      
    // what we're building
    
      
    
        
    A lighting-based puzzle platformer where the player is a lamp. The character itself emits a circle of light that reveals platforms in the dark — players have to navigate by what they can see, planning their next jump before the light moves with them. The core mechanic was validated through 4 rounds of playtesting before we committed scope.
    
        
    I'm running the full development lifecycle:
    
        
      
          
    • 6+ milestones from prototype to Steam release, each with deliverables and gates
      • 
          
    • Weekly playtests with the team and external playtesters
      • 
          
    • UI/UX from menu to HUD to accessibility settings
      • 
          
    • Sound design and adaptive audio cues for the lighting mechanic
      • 
          
    • Narrative scaffolding to give the puzzles dramatic stakes
      •

    // what I learned
    
      
    
        
    Scope is the only real lever. Going into Flikr, I thought the hard part would be coordination. It wasn't — it was saying no. Every milestone we hit happened because we cut something. Every delay came from saying yes to one more "what if we also...". I run scope reviews at every sprint now.
    
        
    Design docs scale teams; opinions don't. Early on I tried to lead the team by being in every Slack thread. It didn't scale past five people. Writing things down — design docs, milestone briefs, rejection criteria — let the team move without me being the bottleneck.
    
        
    Playtest > internal review. We almost shipped a version of the lighting mechanic the whole team thought was good. Four playtest sessions later, we'd quietly redesigned half of it. External users see what insiders can't.

    // the problem
    
      
    
        
    Generic flashcard apps treat every topic the same. They don't know which concepts you're shaky on, when you're free, or what your professor actually emphasized in lecture last week. Real studying is targeted; most tools aren't.
    
        
    I wanted a study tool that actually learns about the learner.
    
      
    
    
    

    
    
      
    // my role
    
      
    
        
    Solo Product & Engineering Lead. Designed the system, built the agent stack, and integrated the learning-science research foundation.
    
      
    
    
    

    
    
      
    // what i built
    
      
    
        
    A multi-agent AI study platform. Several specialized agents coordinate:
    
        
      
          
    • Quiz generator — produces fresh questions based on the user's documented weaknesses
      • 
          
    • Evaluator — analyzes responses and updates a per-user weakness model
      • 
          
    • Mode router — switches between 4 learning modes (recall, application, synthesis, review)
      • 
          
    • Calendar agent — scans Google Calendar to find the user's optimal study windows
      • 
        
    
        
    The whole system is grounded in real learning-science research: pre-trained on 50 papers covering spaced repetition, retrieval practice, interleaving, and metacognition, layered via RAG with the user's per-class materials.

    // what I'm learning
    
      
    
        
    Multi-agent only beats single-agent when each agent does fewer things. My first version had a "super-agent" trying to do everything. The breakthrough was carving out single-purpose agents (Quiz, Eval, Cal, Mode) and letting them coordinate through structured outputs. Counter-intuitively, more agents made the system simpler to debug.
    
        
    RAG quality depends almost entirely on chunking strategy. I assumed embedding model choice would be the lever. Wrong. The 70% gain came from chunking learning-science papers by argument-unit instead of fixed token windows. The model is the easy part; the data is the hard part.
    
        
    Building tools you actually use is the best PRD. I'm a Cornell student. I use Study Bot weekly. Every annoyance is a feature ticket the next morning. If I were building it for someone else's user, I'd be guessing — and guessing is how products end up over-built and under-loved.

      WRITING / THINKING
      3 ESSAYS
      ~5 min read
    
    
    
    How I Think
    
    
    Three short notes on how I run product. Skim them; they're built for it.
    

    
    
      
    
        
    
          01
          prioritization
        
    
        
    Cut scope, not corners.
    
        
    
          Every roadmap I've ever shipped survived because I cut something the team thought was load-bearing. The trick is knowing the difference between scope (the what) and corners (the how). Scope is negotiable; corners aren't. Saying "we'll skip user auth for v0" is scope. Saying "we'll skip QA for v0" is a corner — and it always comes back as a P0 bug at 2am the night before launch.
        
    
        
    
          I pre-commit a scope-cut list at the start of every sprint. When the inevitable pressure hits, the conversation isn't "what do we kill?" — it's "we already agreed."
        
    
      
    

      
    
        
    
          02
          sprints
        
    
        
    Speed-to-learning is the metric.
    
        
    
          At Ventura, we ran weekly outbound A/B tests. Not because A/B testing is sexy — because it was the cheapest way to learn what was true. By the eighth iteration, our reply rate had moved from 1.2% to 4.8% (3-5× industry standard) — but the bigger win was the documented playbook: a list of subject-line patterns, opener moves, and rejection categories that any new SDR could run from day one.
        
    
        
    
          I treat the sprint as a research instrument. If a sprint ships features but no learnings, I count it as half-finished.
        
    
      
    

      
    
        
    
          03
          user research
        
    
        
    Build the tool you use.
    
        
    
          Three of the best products I've shipped — Hillside Trends, Study Bot, Flikr — had me as user-zero. When you're building for someone else's pain point, every PRD is a translation exercise. When you're building for your own, the PRD writes itself and the iteration loop tightens to a day.
        
    
        
    
          The risk is over-fitting to one user (yourself). The fix is interviewing five real users before any feature ships. But the floor of being your own user is much higher than the floor of being adjacent to one.

    Why Claude Code feels different
    
    
    A PM's read on developer-tool design. Anthropic · 2026.
    

    
    
      
    
        Most "AI for developers" products bolt a chat panel onto an editor. Claude Code doesn't. After two months of daily use I think Anthropic figured out something the rest of the category hasn't — and the product decisions that got them there are unusual enough to be worth pulling apart.
      
    

      
    
        
    
          
    1
    
          
    
            
    It commits to the terminal.
    
            
    The product runs in your shell, not your IDE. Most AI dev tools chase IDE integration as the obvious move; Claude Code rejected it. The win: the agent now owns its workspace — file system, git, processes — without inheriting the constraints of a host editor's plugin API. The cost: developers have to learn a new surface. Worth it.
    
          
    
        
    
        
    
          
    2
    
          
    
            
    Long context is a UX feature, not a model spec.
    
            
    The 200k-token context window isn't sold as a "spec" — it's used as permission to be lazy. You don't curate context; you point Claude Code at the repo and trust it to read what matters. That UX bet only works if the underlying capability is genuine. The PM lesson: capability and UX aren't separate. You design them together or not at all.
    
          
    
        
    
        
    
          
    3
    
          
    
            
    Skills + plan-mode are early multi-agent moves.
    
            
    The newer "Skills" and "plan mode" features are the most interesting roadmap signal. Skills look like proto-MCP servers turned into reusable behavior packs. Plan mode is the start of agent-as-PM: stating intent, getting a plan, approving it, then executing. If I were on this team, the next bet I'd make is collaborative plans — multiple humans editing a shared plan before the agent runs.
    
          
    
        
    
        
    
          
    4
    
          
    
            
    The pricing tells you the strategy.
    
            
    Claude Code is bundled into Pro/Max plans, not metered per call. That's not a pricing decision — it's a strategic one. Metering teaches users to use less. Bundling teaches them to use more. For a product that gets better with usage (better context = better answers), bundling is the only honest pricing.
    
          
    
        
    
      
    

      
    
        
    
          What I'd ship next if I were on the Claude Code team: shared agent contexts across a team (I want my teammate's Claude to "remember" the architecture decisions mine just made), and richer plan-mode artifacts (export plans as PRDs, share them in Linear, comment on them async). The product is in a category-defining moment.
